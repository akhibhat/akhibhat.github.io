Pattern Recognition: Humans, AI, ... Astrology?

Insanity is doing the same thing over and over again and expecting different results. - Albert Einstein

Individuals like you and me, who have walked the Earth for quite some time, have a fair understanding of how the world works and how we need to act in a variety of situations.

While waiting to cross an intersection under normal circumstances, it wouldn't surprise us if a car zoomed past just as the light turned yellow. On the other hand, the traffic light pole suddenly disappearing, buildings turning inside out, or people's faces morphing into mythical creatures? Must be a dream or a Marvel movie.

After rigorous lessons and having driven hundreds of kilometers, it becomes second nature to take your foot off the accelerator and step on the brakes when you approach a red light. You subconsciously put on the indicators before you take a turn because, well, those are the rules that you learned. Some of you probably won't even require Google Maps to drive to places that you have already visited before. Your mind knows exactly which flyover to take and which lane to exit from.

But picture this.

You're a kid, maybe of one or two. Crawling on all fours, you reach the kitchen where your parents are scurrying around trying to prepare an elaborate meal for their friends who are about to arrive. Seeing you, mum lifts you up and props you on the kitchen platform so that you don't get hurt in all the commotion. You smell something wonderful nearby, look around and locate the likely source. What do you do?

You touch it.

But the sensation that hits has you confused. You expected it to make you smile, but your tiny hand has turned all red. You turn to look at your mum and start bawling as the pain starts to seep in. Even though you don't really comprehend what just happened, one thing did get clearly ingrained in your brain.

NEVER touch ANYTHING on the kitchen platform. Even if it smells like heaven!!!

I'm sure a lot of you must have had such an experience in your childhood. I'm also sure that never again did you carelessly touch something on the stove with your bare hands.

How did this happen though? How did you go from a baby who touched a hot utensil unknowingly, to knowing the exact moment to step on the brakes to make the vehicle come to a stop in time?

How do humans learn?

Through brain imaging studies, neuroscientists have shown that on a molecular and cellular level, the human brain is remarkably similar to that of non-human primates and lower mammals. It retains many features of the brain structure and cellular organization of birds. Neuroanatomical and neurochemical considerations suggest that the superior intellectual capabilities of humans are largely a result of the increased number of neurons and synapses. For example, mice have 1 billion neurons in their nervous system as compared to humans that have approximately 30 billion only in the brain. It is this huge contrast in numbers and the integrated function of these pattern-processing networks that makes us mentally superior beings.

Basically what this means is that it is not the molecular or cellular structure of our brains that makes us different. It is instead, more of a numerical advantage. As the sheer number of neurons present and the strength of the connections between them increases, we become better at encoding humongous amounts of detailed patterns, integrating all of this information, and making appropriate decisions in pertinent situations.

This is exactly what happens as we age.

At about 18 months, we have all the necessary neurons and far more synapses than required. As we grow further, we perform some actions and perceive the outcome of those actions. This causes specific neurons to fire simultaneously, transmitting signals across the connection between them. The more we experience the same outcome on performing a certain action, stronger the synapses between the respective neurons become. Apart from that, the myelin insulation wrapped around the axons also gets thicker allowing faster transmission of the signals between neurons.

We thus "learn" by associating responses, both environmental and personal, to the actions or events that evoked them. Other neurons and synapses that may not have been stimulated as much during the aging process, get pruned. Unique to each individual, the synaptic connections that remain are a derivative of all influences - genetic, nutritional, toxic, environmental, social, psychological, educational, and even accidental - that have converged, unpredictably and irreproducibly , during the development of that particular brain.

Some of you may already know that techniques used to teach robots to be more "human", like Reinforcement Learning, are based on similar principles.

Pattern Recognition as the basis of AI

In 1943, Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts published a seminal paper that explored the idea of modeling neural activity using formal logical constructs. They proposed a simple model of an artificial neuron - the Perceptron, which later formed the foundation for modern artifical neural networks. They further demonstrated how a network of these artificial neurons could perform complex logical computations.

However, it was in 1950 that Alan Turing asked the question "Can machines think?". He suggested that if humans use available information as well as reason to solve problems and make decisions, then so can machines. In his paper, he discussed how to build such intelligent machines and also gave a way to test their intelligence. Unfortunately at the time, computing was extremely expensive. It took a further five years for a proof of concept - Allen Newell, Cliff Shaw, and Herbert Simon's Logic Theorist to be presented at the Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence.

The symbolic approaches being used in the 1950s, where rules were explicitly programmed, were obviously not perfect and faced a lot of challenges in dealing with real-world uncertainties. Later in the 1980s, there was a shift towards expert systems, where computers were programmed with knowledge from human experts but they struggled when adapting to different domains.

The 1990s marked a significant resurgence in AI with the advent of machine learning and neural networks. Researchers began moving away from rule-based explicitly programmed systems and instead embraced statistical approaches. It was discovered that by arranging layers of interconnected artificial neurons, the perceptrons, these networks could learn and recognize complex patterns in data. This set the stage for the widespread adoption of deep learning, a specific type of neural network architecture with multiple layers, that would significantly improve the ability of AI systems to perform complex tasks.

Fast forward to the present day, in the 21st century, AI is an integral part of our lives. Virtual assistants like Alexa, and Google Home help us do various simple tasks from switching off appliances on command, to automatically sending out Roomba to clean the house. The cars we drive are smart enough to know when to slow down for the red light and when to change lanes to overtake the vehicle in front. Our phones can identify us from our fingerprints and our faces, without having to enter an alphanumeric password.

Let us look at one such example in a little more detail to appreciate how an AI system understands patterns and makes predictions.

How can Google recognize different objects in your images?

If you have the Google Photos app installed on your phone, open it and click on the "Search" tab in the bottom right. You will see sections like Places, Documents, Things and more depending on your settings. View more in the Things section and you will see a screen as below.

You can see that Google has classified all your photos based on the "things" it finds in them. All images clicked near cliffs have been grouped together. The images with statues have been separated from the images with cliffs. But how?

What you cannot see here is that the software has been "trained" by engineers at Google to identify all kinds of things in the world. Let me simplify.

A robot or a computer being able to identify various things in images is not magic, but a meticulously designed algorithm involving multiple steps. First, thousands of images, each, of all the things that the algorithm should be able to identify are collected. Depending on the algorithm chosen, these images are then diligently labeled by the engineers using either of the annotation methods as explained in this blog. One of the methods used is represented below.

Bounding Box showing the coordinates stored (Photo by an_vision on Unsplash)

In the next step, for each image file, a corresponding text file is generated that stores the class name of the "thing" in the image and the coordinates of the box as labeled. For example, the text file for an image with a truck, car and cyclist will be as follows:

Text file containing data about each object in the image

The algorithm consequently goes through each 'image - text file' pair and tries to learn a set of features that are unique to each different class of object that it sees in the images. For example, it might associate features corresponding to "an object floating on water" as a ship or a "pumpkin with a smile" as Halloween. These sets of features, unique to each of the classes, are embedded and stored for later reference.

Now, when you upload a new image on Google Photos, the algorithm will try to extract features from it. Each set of features it extracts will then be compared to the set of features that it already remembers giving us a probability score for each of the unique object it has seen before. The algorithm thus predicts, with a certain probability, the class of the object it found in the new image you uploaded.

Astrology: Basics

It has already been established that the 10 celestial bodies - Sun, Moon, Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, and Pluto - and the 12 constellations - Aries, Taurus, Gemini, Cancer, Leo, Virgo, Libra, Scorpio, Sagittarius, Capricorn, Aquarius, and Pisces - do exist and the data about their positions in the sky is readily available.

Now if we observe from the Earth's perspective, the skies can be perceived as a hollow sphere with all the celestial entities projected on to it. We call this huge globe encompassing the entire Earth, the Celestial Sphere.

We also know that the Earth spins around its axis which is tilted at an angle 23° 47' to the vertical from the West to the East. As a result of this, from the Earth's frame of reference, any point on the Celestial Sphere can be seen to rise from the East and set in the West.

The apparent annual path of the Sun as projected on the Celestial Sphere is called as the Ecliptic. If you observe the path of the other celestial bodies, you will notice that they also move in their individual orbits slightly inclined to the Ecliptic. The interesting thing to note here is that no planet crosses more than 7.5° north or south of the Ecliptic. This belt of 15° has been called the Zodiac. It is also worth noting that the Zodiac gets divided into 12 parts by the previously mentioned 12 constellations that lie in and about this Zodiac belt. This further makes it easier to record the position of the celestial bodies at each time with respect to the constellations.

Before we finally connect everything together we need to locate 2 imaginary, but very important, points in the sky. The Moon's orbit around the Earth as projected on the Celestial Sphere intersects the Ecliptic at 2 distinct points in the sky. These are known as the North node and South node, widely popular to us as Rahu and Ketu respectively.

Patterns in Astrology

After a definitive amount of time, the celestial bodies return to the position that they were once at. Astrologers basically study these motions and help determine how each pattern affects the world as a whole and how the lives of individuals are influenced.
